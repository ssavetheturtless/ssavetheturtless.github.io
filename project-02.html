Can development efforts of 'unbalanced growth' be led by the local entrepreneurs for more sustainable development?

Khee Harvests Coffee

Abstract

This project is born out of an interest in an imbalance between rural development and urban development as well as inclusive peace negotiations and post-conflict policymaking. And also a love for coffee. The contribution to the discourse on 'balanced growth' versus 'unbalanced growth' in development is plenty. And there is research on the involvment of the rural agriculture sector in peace negotiations, particularly in Colombia. This project aims to explore the intersection of the two: can development efforts be led by rural industries and local entrepreneurs if these sectors look promising and can help uplift other sectors, and will this lead to sustained social compacts and policies?

Process

This project has two phases. The first, urgent objective is to support the efforts of ethnic minorities and citizens of Myanmar in their fight against the military junta. During this time, a majority of the profits will be re-directed to medical aid, legal fees, and other necessary funds, decided in consultation with local leaders. The second phase is the core identity of this venture. The current coffee supply-chain has too many unnecessary intermediaries between the farmers and either the roasters or the final sellers, typically coffee shops. The economic value is heavily weighed towards the latter parts of the supply-chain, robbing the coffee farmers of much of the profits for a product they produced. Khee Harvests is setup to identify the farmers as the most essential element to this chain, and reward them in kind.The link between the two phases is where my personal research interest lies. Can these rural farmers living in the mountains inspire more sustainable social compacts by voicing their vision of development and post-conflict policies? And preceding those talks, do these farmers have a role to play in the initial peace negotiations?
